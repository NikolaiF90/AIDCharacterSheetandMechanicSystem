System Guide

Ordinance System

Special abilities, techniques, and moves — executed with mechanical precision, narrated by the AI.

What Is an Ordinance?

An Ordinance is a named special ability — a combat technique, a signature move, a unique skill — that a character can execute during play. When triggered, CSMS rolls dice, detects what happened in the AI's narrative, and applies real mechanical consequences to HP.

The AI writes the story. The script reads what happened and makes it count.

Not just combat. Ordinances can be anything — a hacking technique, a persuasion move, a healing ritual. If your world maker built it as an Ordinance card, it has mechanics behind it.

Executing an Ordinance

Use the /csms ordinance command in your input:

/csms ordinance/CallerName/OrdinanceName/TargetName
PartDescription
CallerNameThe character executing the Ordinance — must have a 📋 sheet
OrdinanceNameThe exact name of the Ordinance — must match the card title
TargetNameThe target character — optional for non-combat Ordinances

Examples

I step forward. /csms ordinance/Alex/Quick Strike/Marcus
Sarah closes her eyes, focusing. /csms ordinance/Sarah/Unwavering Focus
/csms ordinance/Alex/Flashing Shadow/Marcus
The command disappears. Before the AI ever sees your input, CSMS replaces the raw command with natural prose. What the AI reads is something like "execute Quick Strike against Marcus." — no technical clutter in your story feed.

What Happens When You Execute

CSMS runs a multi-step process invisibly behind the scenes:

Step 1 — Classification. The script asks the AI whether the Ordinance is damaging or non-damaging. A strike is damaging. A focus technique is not.

Step 2 — Roll. The AI provides a roll notation (like 2d6) based on the Ordinance card. CSMS rolls the dice and determines the outcome tier — glancing, solid, heavy, fierce, brutal, or lethal.

Step 3 — Narrative & damage. The AI writes the action. CSMS reads the narrative, detects who got hit, resolves pronouns, and applies HP damage accordingly. Both caller and target can take damage depending on what the AI wrote.

The AI narrates freely. CSMS doesn't tell the AI what to write — it reads what the AI wrote and extracts mechanical meaning from it. A clean win means only the target takes damage. A risky exchange means both might.

Assigning Ordinances to Your Character

Your character can only use Ordinances listed in their [Ordinances] block on their character sheet. Open your 📋 card and look at the Notes field:

[Inventory]
- leather jacket
- burner phone

[Ordinances]
- Quick Strike
- Flashing Shadow

If your [Ordinances] block is empty (just a dash), your character can use any Ordinance in the scenario. If it has entries, only those listed are available to you.

Names must match exactly. The Ordinance name in your sheet must match the card title exactly (case insensitive). quick strike and Quick Strike both work — but QuickStrike does not.

When an Ordinance Fails

Two types of failure:

Not assigned. If the Ordinance isn't in your [Ordinances] block, the attempt is blocked before the AI ever sees it. Your input is replaced with a natural failure — the AI narrates the moment as an unsuccessful attempt.

Low roll. If the dice come up poorly, the Ordinance still fires — but the AI's narrative will reflect a weak or failed execution, and damage is applied accordingly (possibly to the caller instead of the target).

For World Makers — Creating an Ordinance Card

An Ordinance lives in a Story Card. The card title becomes the Ordinance name. Here is the full structure:

Card Title

Quick Strike

This is exactly what players type in the command. Keep it clear, memorable, and unique.

Card Keys (Triggers)

Quick Strike, csms_ord

Always include csms_ord as a key — this is how CSMS identifies Ordinance cards. Add the Ordinance name too so it triggers in relevant story context.

Card Entry Field

This is the mechanical definition the AI reads when the Ordinance fires. Be specific — the AI uses this to determine roll notation, damage type, and narrative direction.

Ordinance: Quick Strike
Type: Damaging | Melee
Roll: 2d6
Description: A fast, precise strike aimed at vital points. Prioritizes speed over power — difficult to block, easy to chain into follow-up moves.
On hit: Target staggers or recoils. Caller maintains momentum.
On miss: Caller overextends slightly, leaving a brief opening.
The entry field is your contract with the AI. The more clearly you define the Ordinance — its type, its roll, its narrative flavor — the more consistently the AI will execute it. Vague entries produce inconsistent results.

Non-Damaging Ordinances

Not every Ordinance needs to deal damage. Focus techniques, buffs, debuffs, and support moves work too:

Ordinance: Unwavering Focus
Type: Non-Damaging | Self
Roll: 1d20
Description: The character clears their mind and sharpens their perception. No direct damage — but the narrative should reflect heightened awareness, steadied breathing, and readiness for the next action.
Effect: No HP change. AI describes the mental/physical shift.

Damage Tiers

The dice result maps to a damage tier. Higher tiers deal a larger percentage of the target's max HP:

TierHP RangeNarrative feel
Glancing2% – 6%Graze, brush, clip, scratch
Solid6% – 14%Hit, strike, connect, knock
Heavy14% – 22%Slam, drive, pound, crash
Fierce22% – 35%Maul, cleave, shatter, crush
Brutal35% – 50%Obliterate, pulverize, demolish
Lethal60% – 75%Sever, impale, incinerate, execute

CSMS reads the AI's narrative, finds action words, and maps them to these tiers. The AI doesn't need to know the tiers exist — it writes naturally, and the script extracts meaning from what it wrote.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need a target?

Only for damaging Ordinances. Non-damaging Ordinances like buffs or focus techniques don't need a target — just omit the last argument:

/csms ordinance/Alex/Unwavering Focus

Can multiple characters have the same Ordinance?

Yes. The Ordinance card exists once in the scenario — any character with it listed in their [Ordinances] block can use it.

What if my Ordinance isn't on my sheet?

The attempt fails before the AI sees it. The input is replaced with natural failure prose — the AI narrates the moment as an unsuccessful attempt. Nothing breaks.

Can the caller take damage too?

Yes. If the AI's narrative describes a counter-attack or the caller getting hit, CSMS detects it and applies damage to the caller as well. The script reads what actually happened — not what you intended.
