System Guide

Ordinance System

Special abilities, techniques, and moves — executed with mechanical precision, narrated by the AI.

What Is an Ordinance?

An Ordinance is a named special ability — a combat technique, a signature move, a unique skill — that a character can execute during play. When triggered, CSMS rolls dice, detects what happened in the AI's narrative, and applies real mechanical consequences to HP.

The AI writes the story. The script reads what happened and makes it count.

Not just combat. Ordinances can be anything — a hacking technique, a persuasion move, a healing ritual. If your world maker built it as an Ordinance card, it has mechanics behind it.

Executing an Ordinance

Use the /csms ordinance command in your input:

/csms ordinance/CallerName/OrdinanceName/TargetName
PartDescription
CallerNameThe character executing the Ordinance — must have a 📋 sheet
OrdinanceNameThe exact name of the Ordinance — must match the card title
TargetNameThe target character — optional for non-combat Ordinances

Examples

I step forward. /csms ordinance/Alex/Quick Strike/Marcus
Sarah closes her eyes, focusing. /csms ordinance/Sarah/Unwavering Focus
/csms ordinance/Alex/Flashing Shadow/Marcus
The command disappears. Before the AI ever sees your input, CSMS replaces the raw command with natural prose. What the AI reads is something like "execute Quick Strike against Marcus." — no technical clutter in your story feed.

What Happens When You Execute

CSMS runs a multi-step process invisibly behind the scenes:

Step 1 — Classification. The script asks the AI whether the Ordinance is damaging or non-damaging. A strike is damaging. A focus technique is not.

Step 2 — Roll. The AI provides a roll notation (like 2d6) based on the Ordinance card. CSMS rolls the dice and determines the outcome tier — glancing, solid, heavy, fierce, brutal, or lethal.

Step 3 — Narrative & damage. The AI writes the action. CSMS reads the narrative, detects who got hit, resolves pronouns, and applies HP damage accordingly. Both caller and target can take damage depending on what the AI wrote.

The AI narrates freely. CSMS doesn't tell the AI what to write — it reads what the AI wrote and extracts mechanical meaning from it. A clean win means only the target takes damage. A risky exchange means both might.

Assigning Ordinances to Your Character

Your character can only use Ordinances listed in their [Ordinances] block on their character sheet. Open your 📋 card and look at the Notes field:

[Inventory]
- leather jacket
- burner phone

[Ordinances]
- Quick Strike
- Flashing Shadow

If your [Ordinances] block is empty (just a dash), your character can use any Ordinance in the scenario. If it has entries, only those listed are available to you.

Names must match exactly. The Ordinance name in your sheet must match the card title exactly (case insensitive). quick strike and Quick Strike both work — but QuickStrike does not.

When an Ordinance Fails

Two types of failure:

Not assigned. If the Ordinance isn't in your [Ordinances] block, the attempt is blocked before the AI ever sees it. Your input is replaced with a natural failure — the AI narrates the moment as an unsuccessful attempt.

Low roll. If the dice come up poorly, the Ordinance still fires — but the AI's narrative will reflect a weak or failed execution, and damage is applied accordingly (possibly to the caller instead of the target).

For World Makers — Creating an Ordinance Card

An Ordinance lives in a Story Card. Here is exactly how to set one up:

Step 1 — Set the Card Type

When creating the card, set Type to Custom. A new empty field will appear below it — type Ordinance there. This is how CSMS identifies the card as an Ordinance. Without this, the command won't find it.

Type must be exactly "Ordinance". Capitalization doesn't matter, but spelling does. If CSMS can't find any Ordinance cards, it will tell you to check this field.

Step 2 — Name the Card

Quick Strike

This is exactly what players type in the command. Keep it clear, memorable, and unique. The match is case insensitive — quick strike and Quick Strike both work.

Step 3 — Write the Entry Field

This is what the AI reads when the Ordinance fires. You don't need to include roll notation, damage type, or DAMAGING/NONDAMAGING labels — the AI already understands from the description. Keep it short and precise:

A lightning-fast strike delivered before the opponent can react. Prioritizes speed over power.

That's enough. The AI knows it's an attack. It knows how to narrate it. More detail is fine — but only if it helps the AI understand something it wouldn't otherwise:

Ordinance: Flashing Shadow
A burst of explosive movement closing distance instantly. High risk — if it misses, the caller is left exposed.
Roll: 3d6
Less is often more. Most AI models respond better to short, precise instructions than long detailed ones. Write what's unique about the Ordinance — not everything that could happen. And remember: results depend on the AI model. Some follow instructions more reliably than others.

Step 4 — Add Triggers

Triggers are keywords that bring the card into AI context when mentioned in the story. Add the Ordinance name at minimum:

Damage Tiers

The dice result maps to a damage tier. Higher tiers deal a larger percentage of the target's max HP:

TierHP RangeNarrative feel
Glancing2% – 6%Graze, brush, clip, scratch
Solid6% – 14%Hit, strike, connect, knock
Heavy14% – 22%Slam, drive, pound, crash
Fierce22% – 35%Maul, cleave, shatter, crush
Brutal35% – 50%Obliterate, pulverize, demolish
Lethal60% – 75%Sever, impale, incinerate, execute

CSMS reads the AI's narrative, finds action words, and maps them to these tiers. The AI doesn't need to know the tiers exist — it writes naturally, and the script extracts meaning from what it wrote.

Credit in the Notes field. The Notes field on a story card is visible to players during character creation but invisible to the AI — a clean place to put a credit line without affecting how the Ordinance performs.

Frequently Asked Questions

The AI seems to ignore my Ordinance. What's happening?

This happens occasionally and depends heavily on the model you're using. Some models follow story card instructions more reliably than others. If it happens, don't panic — clear the last AI output and your input, then retype the command and try again. Sometimes the model just needs another shot at it.

What AI models work best with Ordinances?

The system has been fully tested with Harbinger (24B) — a model tuned specifically for high stakes, meaningful choices, and harsh consequences. Harbinger doesn't care if you're the protagonist. It raises the stakes and makes every decision count, which pairs extremely well with the Ordinance system's mechanical weight.

Wayfarer Small 2 (12B) is also a strong candidate — focused entirely on combat, injury, and high stakes consequences. Untested with CSMS directly, but the design philosophy aligns well. Wayfarer Large shares the same focus and is similarly promising but untested.

Are there models that won't work?

A few categories to be cautious with:

  • Rotating test models — Dynamic Small and similar unstable models are unpredictable. Not recommended.
  • Slice of life models — Models not designed for conflict or action may refuse to narrate violence or simply ignore combat mechanics entirely. Hermes 3 70B is one example.
  • Caching models — Raven (357B/32B) and Atlas (671B/37B) are untested. It's unclear whether caching models work with CSMS at all. Proceed with caution and expect surprises.

Where can I find Ordinances to use in my scenario?

Honestly — there's no central library yet. Someone may open a thread on the AI Dungeon Discord, or one may appear in the future. In the meantime, feel free to reach out directly on Discord: princef90.

My Ordinance failed. What do I do?

Depends on the type of failure:

  • Low roll result — that's narrative. Accept it, or clear the last output and input and try again.
  • AI narrated something off — rewrite your Ordinance entry. Shorter and more precise usually fixes it.
  • AI ignored it entirely — clear the last output and input, retype the command, try again. Models sometimes need a second attempt.

I cleared the output but the Ordinance is still running through its steps.

This is normal. The system uses a multi-step method to execute Ordinances — it tracks which step it's currently on. If you delete or edit the last AI output mid-process, the script doesn't know that happened. You need to let the full sequence complete before clearing. If you're stuck mid-step, just continue through to the end, then clear from there.

Do I need to assign every Ordinance to my character?

Only if your world maker has set it up that way. If your [Ordinances] block is empty (just a dash), you can use any Ordinance in the scenario. If it has entries, only those listed are available to you.

Do I need a target?

Only for damaging Ordinances. Non-damaging Ordinances like buffs or focus techniques don't need a target — just omit the last argument:

/csms ordinance/Alex/Unwavering Focus

Can multiple characters have the same Ordinance?

Yes. The Ordinance card exists once in the scenario — any character with it listed in their [Ordinances] block can use it.

Can the caller take damage too?

Yes. If the AI's narrative describes a counter-attack or the caller getting hit, CSMS detects it and applies damage to the caller as well. The script reads what actually happened — not what you intended.
